Headline read — where Denver sits in context
Denver delivered registration volume in line with target but show rate at 10.5%, three points below the trailing 2026 series average of 13.42% and nine points below the 2025 baseline of 19.35%. The close rate of 23.4% is healthy and above the buy rate threshold the client uses internally. Read together: the people who walked through the door bought at a strong rate. The bottleneck is upstream of the door, not at the close. This is the same pattern we have been calling out at the portfolio level and the Denver data confirms it on a single-market read.

The Loveland model — what to replicate
Format signal
Lunch slot, small venue
Tuesday lunch, Embassy Suites Loveland. Smallest registration base in the market at 89, but the highest show rate (14.6%) and the highest close rate (46.2%) by a wide margin.
Repeatable pattern
High-intent / low-volume
Lower volume registrations correlate with higher intent. Lunch slots filter for committed weekday participation. Small venue keeps the room intimate and the close mechanic warm.
Recommendation: for the next Colorado-style market, slot at least one lunchtime, small-capacity venue at a Loveland-equivalent location. Treat it as the market anchor and protect its budget allocation. The Loveland reg-to-buyer rate (6.7%) is more than double the Denver-metro average (2.6%) and equal to what we want every venue to look like.
The Denver DTC anomaly — biggest base, worst show rate, strong close
Denver DTC pulled 231 registrations, the largest base in the market. Show rate dropped to 7.4%, the second-worst in Denver. But of the 17 who attended, six bought. That is a 35.3% close rate, the second-best in the market. So the registration mechanic was strong (volume came in), the close mechanic was strong (those who showed bought), but the in-between step failed.
Likely driver: evening slot + downtown audience + largest base. Three factors compound. Largest base means the average registrant intent is lower (volume regression). Downtown evening venues compete with after-work plans. The DTC audience over-indexes on professionals who registered with intent but rescheduled with low cost. We are not pushing budget down, we are pushing it to the wrong shape of registrant.
Recommendation: on next high-volume urban venue, test capping registrations earlier in the window to lift average intent. Aim for a smaller absolute reg count with a tighter intent profile. The DTC close rate proves the buyer is there. We just need to get more of them to show up.
The Aurora failure — funnel worked, close did not
Aurora is the clearest single-venue read of where Denver lost money. The market delivered 24 attendees, second only to Colorado Springs Friday. But only one converted. That is a 4.2% close rate. By comparison, every other Denver-metro venue cleared 22%. The paid media side did its job. Something in the room failed.
Hypotheses to investigate before the June 13 Aurora rebooking:
  • Speaker delivery. Was a specific speaker assigned to Aurora that is different from the Loveland or Denver DTC speakers? Compare attribution rate by speaker if data is available.
  • Audience-message fit. Was the registrant profile pulled by paid media materially different in Aurora vs the rest of Denver metro? Cross-check Meta audience composition at the campaign level.
  • Venue logistics. Room layout, seating capacity, A/V quality, signage. Any operational variable that could shape how engaged the room was during the close.
  • Time of day / day of week. Aurora day not specified in the source report. If it was the same day as a higher-converting event, the lateral pull on the room is a candidate.
Recommendation: do not rebook Aurora for June 13 without addressing at least one of the hypotheses above. The risk is the same execution pattern repeating with a fresh audience. Lower-risk path: swap Aurora for a different metro venue with comparable demographics, hold the Aurora seat for September.
Colorado Springs regional drag
Both Colorado Springs slots (Friday 13.2% close, Saturday 5.4% close) underperformed Denver-metro venues. Combined they delivered 4 buyers across 244 registrations, a 1.6% reg-to-buyer rate against the Denver-metro 3.5%. The Friday slot did better on attendance but worse on close. The Saturday slot did poorly on both.
The Friday vs Saturday split inside Springs is informative. Friday pulled enough volume (151 regs, 20 attendees) but the close fell apart at 15%. Saturday pulled less volume (93 regs, 5 attendees) and the close was barely better (20%). This is not a slot-quality problem, it is a market-fit problem. The same creative and audience pull that works in Denver-metro Loveland and Highlands Ranch did not translate to the Springs market.
  • Consolidate the Springs Saturday slot into the Friday slot for the next Colorado launch. Saturday's reg-to-buyer of 1.1% does not justify the venue cost.
  • Springs-specific creative test. Brian-themed video performed well in Denver-metro. Test a Brian-Springs cut for the next launch. If the gap closes, the issue is messaging localization. If it does not, Springs may simply be a weaker market for this offer.
  • Audience tightening. Springs likely needs a different interest layer than Denver-metro. Test wealth-focused interest audiences narrower to higher-income segments.

The Denver BOFU inversion is a market-warmth signal. Implication for the portfolio: we can treat BOFU share as a "warmth score" to predict registration mix in the next launch. Markets where we have been live longer (Cincinnati, San Diego repeat exposure since April) should show higher BOFU share in attribution. Markets going live for the first time (Greenville) should expect TOFU to carry most of the close volume.
UTM hygiene gap — process to fix this week
Three of 22 attributed buyers were tagged with the Fresno May 4 campaign, but two of them had Denver-specific creative (Brian-Denver). This is either genuine demand-gen lookalike overlap from Fresno, or a UTM mis-tagging issue. The Meta Auto-Tagged single buyer (Colorado Springs Saturday) confirms a manual UTM tagging discipline gap. Four total buyers (18% of attributed) sit in attribution noise that should be cleanable.
  • Enforce naming convention before next launch: campaign name must include the market code (e.g. `[SG] - 20260524_GVL - ...`) at the campaign level, not just the ad set or creative.
  • Audit existing campaign UTMs on May 22 before the Sunday launch. Any campaign with cross-market creative tags gets a relabel before going live.
  • No campaign reuse across markets. Even if the audience is identical, spin up a new campaign per market so UTM lineage is clean.
